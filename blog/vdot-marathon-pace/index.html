VDOT marathon pace explained

VDOT is a practical way to translate race performances into a “fitness value” that can guide training intensities and estimate equivalent performances at other distances. Used correctly, it can help you choose a realistic marathon pace. Used blindly, it can overestimate your marathon.

Published: 2026-01-29 • Updated: 2026-01-29

What is VDOT (in plain English)?

VDOT is associated with Jack Daniels’ training framework. It uses race performance to estimate a runner’s current capability and prescribe training paces. The key idea: race time reflects a blend of VO₂max, running economy, and endurance — so a race can be used as a practical “fitness marker.”

Good explanations: Coaches Education (Daniels article), RunDNA overview.

Why VDOT can mis-predict your marathon

  • Marathon-specific durability: your ability to hold pace late depends on training volume, long runs, and fueling practice.
  • Race selection: short races can overemphasize speed; longer races (half marathon) usually predict marathon better.
  • Execution & conditions: heat/hills/wind can force effort-based pacing.
Better predictor for marathon? Use the half marathon method: Marathon pace from half marathon time.

How to use VDOT safely for marathon pacing

  1. Start with your longest recent race. Half marathon is usually best; 10K can work; 5K is riskier.
  2. Convert to a marathon target range, not a single number. If your training base is limited, choose the conservative end.
  3. Build a checkpoint plan. Use marathon splits to avoid early errors.
  4. Prefer negative split execution. See negative split pacing.

Practical alternative: use your race + a prediction model

If you want the same “VDOT idea” without relying on tables, use a race predictor with conservative assumptions:

  • 10K → marathon pace
  • Half → marathon pace
  • Race predictor (combine multiple races)
