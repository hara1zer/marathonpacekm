Marathon pace from 10K time

Your 10K is a strong signal of fitness, but it’s still much shorter than a marathon — so the key is using a model that accounts for the way pace typically slows as distance increases. Below is a practical calculator and realistic ranges.

The prediction model (simple, widely used)

A common race-prediction approach is a power-law relationship between time and distance, popularized by Pete Riegel. In its classic form, it uses an exponent around 1.06 for endurance events.

Reference: Riegel’s “Athletic Records and Human Endurance” (American Scientist) is frequently cited as the basis for this style of model. See open copies: RRTC PDF or alternate PDF.

10K → marathon calculator

Tip: if your long-run base is limited, choose a more conservative exponent (1.07–1.08). If you have strong endurance and marathon-specific training, 1.05–1.06 may be closer.

Why 10K-based predictions can miss

  • Endurance vs speed: a good 10K reflects fitness, but marathon success also depends on durability, fueling, and pacing.
  • Heat/hills/wind: conditions can change marathon pace meaningfully — plan by effort when needed.
  • Execution: even small early mistakes can cause large late slowdowns.
