Half Marathon to Marathon Pace: How to Convert Your Time Accurately

Your half marathon time is one of the most powerful tools for predicting marathon performance — if you convert it correctly.

Many runners make the same mistake: they double their half marathon time and assume that’s a realistic marathon goal. For most runners, this leads to aggressive pacing, late-race fatigue, and a painful final 10 km.

This guide explains how to convert your half marathon time into a realistic marathon pace, with clear ranges, examples, and adjustments based on mileage and experience.

Why the Half Marathon Is the Best Predictor

The half marathon balances speed and endurance better than any other race distance.

  • Shorter races (5K, 10K) overestimate marathon ability
  • The marathon itself is too dependent on fueling and durability

A well-raced half marathon reflects aerobic capacity, fatigue resistance, and pacing control — all critical for marathon success.

Why You Can’t Just Double Your Half Marathon Time

The marathon introduces physiological stress that doesn’t fully appear in a half marathon:

  • Glycogen depletion
  • Muscle fiber damage
  • Heat accumulation
  • Neuromuscular fatigue

Even well-trained runners experience some slowdown in the second half of the marathon.

The key question is not if you slow down — but how much.

Typical Half Marathon → Marathon Slowdown

Most runners fall into one of these ranges:

  • Elite / very high mileage: +3–5%
  • Well-trained amateurs: +6–10%
  • First-time or low mileage runners: +10–15%

The better your endurance base, the smaller the slowdown.

How Weekly Mileage Changes the Conversion

Two runners with identical half marathon times may need very different marathon pacing.

General guidance:

  • Under 50 km/week: use conservative end of the range
  • 60–75 km/week: mid-range targets are realistic
  • 80+ km/week: aggressive but controlled pacing possible

Mileage builds the durability required to hold pace after 30 km.

Why Long Runs Matter More Than Speed

The best indicator that your conversion is realistic is how your long runs feel.

Signs your marathon pace is appropriate:

  • You can finish 28–32 km runs without dramatic slowing
  • Marathon-pace segments feel controlled, not forced
  • You recover well in the days following long runs

If marathon pace feels unsustainable in training, it will not improve on race day.

Common Half-to-Marathon Conversion Mistakes

  • Using an old half marathon result
  • Ignoring weekly mileage
  • Overestimating race-day adrenaline
  • Choosing the fastest possible conversion

When in doubt, choose the slower end of the range.
