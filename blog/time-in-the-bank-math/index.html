The Math of “Time in the Bank”

Lots of runners try to “bank time” early (e.g., 10 sec/km faster than goal pace) and hope to hang on. Here’s the problem: the math is brutal. A surprisingly tiny slowdown later can erase all your early gains.

Published: 2026-01-28 • Updated: 2026-01-28
Use this with your pacing plan Plug your goal time into the Marathon Pace Calculator, then use the break-even math below to sanity-check your early pacing.

The one equation that kills “banking time”

Suppose you run the first D km Δ seconds per km faster than goal pace. You “gain” Δ × D seconds early. If you then slow by S seconds per km for the remaining distance, you “lose” S × (42.195 − D) seconds.

Break-even slowdown

The slowdown that wipes out your early gains is: 

Break-even S (sec/km) = (Δ × D) / (42.195 − D)

If your actual slowdown later is bigger than this, “banking time” makes you slower overall.

What real marathon data says about pacing (and “the wall”)

  • Large-scale analyses of marathon splits consistently find that faster runners pace more evenly than slower runners. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
  • A big open-access study using millions of race records defined “hitting the wall” as sustained late-race slowing and found it’s common (especially among men), typically late in the race. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
  • Physiological models show that as intensity rises, the predicted distance to complete glycogen depletion falls, with failure risk often clustering around the later miles if fueling/intensity aren’t managed. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}
  • A systematic review of marathon pacing research notes positive pacing is common, and highlights practical value in understanding pacing strategy selection. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
Translation: “banking time” is a fragile bet. The math says you can’t afford much late slowdown. Real-world marathon data shows late slowdowns are common — especially when pacing is aggressive. So the safest path to a PR is usually: controlled start → settle → only push after 30–32K if you still feel good.

3 simple rules to avoid the 30–35K blow-up

  1. Start controlled for 3–5K. Your HR/RPE should feel “too easy.” (You can’t win the race here — you can only lose it.)
  2. Lock into goal effort, not ego. If conditions are tough (heat/wind/hills), pace by effort and accept the time adjustment.
  3. Earn the right to speed up after 30K. If you’ve fueled well and your effort is steady, then gradually lift pace.
