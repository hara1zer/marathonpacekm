How to Choose a Realistic Marathon Goal Time

Choosing the right marathon goal time is one of the most important decisions you’ll make in your entire training cycle. Get it right, and the race feels controlled, challenging, and rewarding. Get it wrong, and even great fitness can end in a painful final 10 kilometres.

Most marathon blow-ups don’t happen because runners aren’t fit enough. They happen because the goal time was unrealistic.

This guide will help you choose a marathon goal time you can actually hold — based on fitness, mileage, and durability, not optimism.

Why Marathon Goal Times Are So Often Wrong

The marathon punishes small mistakes. A goal pace that’s just 5–10 seconds per kilometre too fast may feel fine early, but compounds brutally after 30 km.

Common reasons runners choose unrealistic goals:

  • Basing goals on short races instead of endurance
  • Doubling half marathon time without adjustment
  • Copying friends or online calculators blindly
  • Ignoring heat, course profile, or fueling

A realistic goal time is not pessimistic — it’s strategic.

The 4 Inputs That Actually Matter

1. Recent Race Performance

Your best indicators are:

  • Half marathon (most reliable)
  • 10K (use with caution)

A recent half marathon run close to maximal effort provides an excellent starting point. For most runners, marathon pace is 6–12% slower than half marathon pace.

Example:

  • 1:30 half → realistic marathon range: 3:10–3:15 (3:10 pace)
  • 1:40 half → realistic marathon range: 3:30–3:35 (3:30 pace)

2. Weekly Mileage (This Matters More Than You Think)

Two runners with identical half marathon times but different weekly mileage should not target the same marathon pace.

General guidance:

  • 40–55 km/week: conservative end of pace range
  • 60–75 km/week: mid-range pace possible
  • 80+ km/week: aggressive but controlled pacing

Mileage builds durability. Without it, pace collapses late.

3. Long-Run Endurance

Your longest runs matter more than your fastest workouts.

Strong marathon readiness includes:

  • Regular runs of 28–32 km
  • Some long runs with marathon-pace segments
  • Ability to finish long runs without dramatic slowdown

If your long runs fall apart after 25 km, your goal pace is too ambitious.

4. Race-Day Conditions

Heat, humidity, wind, and hills all demand pace adjustment.

If conditions are challenging, adjust expectations early — not at 32 km.

A cooler day might allow 3:30 pace. A hot or hilly course may require 3:45 pace instead.

Use A / B / C Goals (Smart Runners Do This)

Elite and experienced marathoners rarely commit to a single rigid goal.

  • A Goal: Perfect execution, ideal conditions
  • B Goal: Realistic, repeatable performance
  • C Goal: Finish strong and learn

Your pacing plan should be based on your B goal, not your dream outcome.

Example Goal-Setting Scenarios

Scenario 1

  • Half marathon: 1:45
  • Weekly mileage: ~60 km

Recommended goal: 3:45 marathon pace

Scenario 2

  • Half marathon: 1:45
  • Weekly mileage: 80–90 km

Recommended goal: 3:30 marathon pace

Common Marathon Goal Time Mistakes

  • Doubling half marathon time without slowdown
  • Ignoring fueling practice
  • Training well but pacing poorly
  • Letting ego choose the goal

The marathon rewards restraint far more than bravado.
